Milestone Guide: Measurement and Your Proposal

This page walks you through each of this week's four milestones. For each one you will find guidance, a worked example, and a place to practice. Work through them in order. By the time you post in the discussion forum, you should have a concrete draft of each piece.

Milestone 1

What you need to do

Operationalize your key variables

Write precise definitions of each construct in your study as it will actually be measured. Not what it means in theory — how you will capture it in data.

Every construct in your study needs two definitions: a conceptual definition (what it means in theory) and an operational definition (exactly how you will measure it). Here is the difference:

Example: Relationship Satisfaction

Conceptual: Relationship satisfaction is a person's overall subjective evaluation of their romantic partnership, including perceptions of closeness, communication quality, and commitment.

Operational: Relationship satisfaction will be measured using the Relationship Assessment Scale (RAS; Hendrick, 1988), a 7-item self-report instrument scored on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = low satisfaction, 5 = high satisfaction). Total scores range from 7 to 35, with higher scores indicating greater satisfaction.

Milestone 2

What you need to do

Write a clear, APA formatted Purpose Statement

One or two sentences that announce what your study intends to investigate, who the participants are, and what the key variables are. This sentence goes near the end of your Introduction section.

A purpose statement follows a predictable structure in APA writing. Shehzad (2011) identifies three moves: announcing the intent, naming the variables or phenomena, and identifying the population. Use the template below to draft yours.

Fill in the Blanks

The purpose of this study is to and its relationship to among .

See a model example ↓

Model Purpose Statement

The purpose of this quantitative study is to examine parenting stress and its relationship to child behavioral outcomes among parents of children with autism spectrum disorder between the ages of 3 and 12 years.

Notice: design type named, key variables named, population clearly identified, written as a single declarative sentence.

Milestone 3

What you need to do

Continue building your literature base

Keep reading sources closely and taking detailed notes. By the end of this week you should have 12 to 15 peer reviewed sources collected and at least 8 read closely enough to summarize.

Reading closely means more than skimming the abstract. For each article you plan to cite, you should be able to answer these four questions:

Four Questions for Every Source

1. What did they study? — population, design, key variables

2. What did they find? — key results in plain language

3. What does it mean for my study? — how it informs your research question

4. What theme does it belong to? — which section of your literature review will it anchor?

Milestone 4

What you need to do

Know which scales you plan to use and why

Before Week 7's instrument building week, you need to identify at least two validated scales for your key variables and be able to justify why each one fits your study.

A validated scale is one that has published evidence for its reliability and validity. Below are some commonly used scales in MFT and human sciences research. Click any card to see details. This is not an exhaustive list — use it as a starting point, then search PsycTests or the original articles in your topic area.

Dyadic Adjustment Scale (DAS)

Constructs: Relationship quality, dyadic consensus, satisfaction

Items: 32 items  •  Format: Mixed response formats
Population: Married and cohabiting couples
Citation: Spanier (1976). Journal of Marriage and the Family.
Note: One of the most widely used couple measures in MFT research.

Relationship Assessment Scale (RAS)

Constructs: Relationship satisfaction

Items: 7 items  •  Format: 5-point Likert
Population: Adults in romantic relationships
Citation: Hendrick (1988). Journal of Marriage and the Family.
Note: Brief and widely validated. Good choice for survey research.

Experiences in Close Relationships (ECR)

Constructs: Attachment anxiety, attachment avoidance

Items: 36 items  •  Format: 7-point Likert
Population: Adults in romantic relationships
Citation: Brennan, Clark, and Shaver (1998).
Note: Strong psychometric evidence across many samples. ECR-R is a revised version.

Parenting Stress Index (PSI)

Constructs: Parenting stress, parent-child relationship stress

Items: 120 items (short form: 36)  •  Format: 5-point Likert
Population: Parents of children ages 0 to 12
Citation: Abidin (1995).
Note: Well validated for clinical and research use. Short form widely used.

PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire)

Constructs: Depression severity

Items: 9 items  •  Format: 4-point frequency scale
Population: General adult population; widely used in clinical settings
Citation: Kroenke, Spitzer, and Williams (2001). Journal of General Internal Medicine.
Note: Free to use. Excellent psychometric properties across diverse samples.

GAD-7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder Scale)

Constructs: Anxiety severity

Items: 7 items  •  Format: 4-point frequency scale
Population: General adult population
Citation: Spitzer et al. (2006). Archives of Internal Medicine.
Note: Free to use. Widely validated companion to the PHQ-9.

Family Assessment Device (FAD)

Constructs: Family functioning, problem solving, communication, roles

Items: 60 items  •  Format: 4-point Likert
Population: Family members age 12 and older
Citation: Epstein, Baldwin, and Bishop (1983).
Note: Based on the McMaster Model of Family Functioning. Subscales can be used independently.

OLSAT / FACES (Cohesion and Flexibility)

Constructs: Family cohesion, family flexibility

Items: 20 items (FACES-IV)  •  Format: 5-point Likert
Population: Adolescents and adults
Citation: Olson (2011). Journal of Marital and Family Therapy.
Note: Based on the Circumplex Model. FACES-IV addresses earlier limitations of FACES-III.

How to justify your scale choice

When you write your Measurement Rationale in Week 7, for each scale you need to answer four questions:

1. Why this scale? — How does it match your construct and research question?

2. What is the reliability evidence? — Report Cronbach's alpha or test-retest reliability from the original validation paper.

3. What is the validity evidence? — What types of validity have been established? (Construct, content, criterion?)

4. How will you score it? — Sum the items? Reverse score any? What does a high score mean?
